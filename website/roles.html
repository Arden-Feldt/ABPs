Research/Programming

National Defense Science and Engineering Graduate (NDSEG) Research Fellow: Computational Physics (Active Matter)

UNC-Chapel Hill: Klotsa Group

Using C++ and molecular dynamics, I simulate active matter systems of self-propelled particles that phase separate. Using algorithms I write in Python, I analyze big data to understand how properties of the individual agents contribute to the dynamic, collective behavior of the whole. Finally, I derive novel mathematical theories predicated on fundamental physics that explain and predict the observed collective behavior.


One challenge was navigating the interdisciplinary nature of expectations of capabilities to perform this research. I designed, built, and verified novel measurement systems to enable this research, such as a tool that measures the minimum intensity of detectible light. This was particularly challenging due to the measurement's incredible sensitivity to noise, so I had to be creative and precise with extreme noise mitigation techniques to make an accurate and user-friendly tool.


Successes include proving how self-propulsion modifies the steady-state force balance in our phase-separated system, deriving an analytical theory that can predict the structural properties (density, pressure, size, and lattice spacing) of the steady state solely from our simulation inputs (experimentally measurable properties), and mentoring new graduate and undergraduate students with multiple research projects. A grant proposal I wrote for this project was awarded the prestigious Department of Defense (DoD) National Defense Science and Engineering Graduate (NDSEG) Fellowship to fully fund three years of my graduate career.

Research Intern: Computational Physics (Fusion Plasmas) DONE

Wisconsin Plasma Physics Laboratory (WiPPL)

Based upon an older IDL model, I developed a computational model in Python that accurately predicts the energy emissions from fusion plasma generators. By applying my computational model to determine the theoretically optimal design, I fabricated an x-ray detector that removes undesirable noise from other measurements, effectively increasing the measurement capabilities of scientists.


Before this position, I had never seen a line of computer code. Within this short period of time, I taught myself how to read and write complex code in both Python and IDL and applied this knowledge to design a physics-based, deterministic model, whose accuracy was successfully verified experimentally. One challenge I experienced was verifying my physical detector, with noisier, experimental data, agreed with a highly precise, deterministic computational model. I utilized statistics to conclude that our detector was behaving as we predicted.


Successes included increasing the Python model’s efficiency by 60% through by replacing 1D lists and iterations over a loop with up to 6-dimensional tensor mathematics via NumPy, adding the capability to calculate the elemental impurity concentrations in plasmas and predict the measured signal of x-ray detectors, and developing multi-variable optimization routines to maximize the capabilities of x-ray detectors being designed with the computational model.
